# Hypothermia First Aid

> ⚠️ Hackathon demo sample content. For severe hypothermia, call 911 / 112 / local emergency immediately.

## Identify the severity

Hypothermia has three stages. **Mild** (core 32-35°C / 90-95°F): violent shivering, cold limbs, slowed reactions. **Moderate** (28-32°C / 82-90°F): shivering stops, slurred speech, unsteady gait, drowsiness. **Severe** (<28°C / <82°F): loss of consciousness, irregular heartbeat, life-threatening. Cessation of shivering is a **deterioration signal, not recovery**.

## Get out of the cold

The first step is always to **escape wind and rain — get into a tent, behind a rock, or under dense trees**. Wind chill makes heat loss several times faster than the air temperature suggests. Even above-freezing temperatures with strong wind and wet clothing can cause hypothermia.

## Remove wet clothing

Wet clothing conducts heat 25× faster than dry clothing. In a sheltered spot, **strip off all wet clothes immediately** and replace with dry layers. If no spare clothes, wring out existing ones before re-wearing and wrap with foil blanket or sleeping bag.

## Using emergency foil blanket

The reflective side of the foil blanket **faces the body** to reflect body heat back. Wrapping order: torso first, then head (head loses 30% of body heat), then limbs. Foil blanket is **insulation, not heating** — alone it is insufficient for severe hypothermia and needs to be combined with other warm layers.

## Warm drinks

If the patient is **conscious and able to swallow**, give warm sweetened drinks (hot cocoa, sugar water, sports drinks). **No alcohol**: alcohol dilates blood vessels and accelerates heat loss. **No caffeine**: it causes dehydration. Never feed an unconscious patient — high risk of aspiration.

## Things NOT to do

Do not **apply direct heat or open fire to extremities** — peripheral vasodilation sends cold blood back to the heart, potentially causing fatal arrhythmia. Do not **vigorously rub or massage limbs** — same reason. Do not **let the patient sleep alone**; maintain conversation to keep them alert.

## When to SOS

Call SOS the moment the patient shows **moderate hypothermia signs** (shivering stops, slurred speech, drowsy). In wilderness, moderate hypothermia can progress to severe within 1-2 hours and requires medical equipment to reverse. While waiting, **two rescuers performing skin-to-skin warming** of the patient's torso is an effective emergency measure.
