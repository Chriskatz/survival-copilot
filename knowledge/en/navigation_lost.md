# Lost in Wilderness — Action Protocol

> ⚠️ Hackathon demo sample content. If actually lost, prioritize calling 911 / 112 and using any available emergency signaling device.

## The STOP rule

The moment you realize you are lost, execute STOP: **Stop, Think, Observe, Plan**. Studies show lost hikers who keep moving expand their search radius, exhaust energy, and reduce their chance of being found. Stop and take 10 deep breaths to let panic subside.

## Do NOT keep walking

The most dangerous reaction is to **"go downhill" or "follow a stream"**. Mountain terrain in Taiwan and similar regions is fragmented — streams flow into waterfalls and gorges with extremely high fatality rates. The correct response is to **stay put** and let rescuers come to you, not chase you.

## Assess current location

Recall when you last confirmed you were on the trail. Observe terrain features: slope direction, vegetation, any man-made markers (trail tape, paint marks, plastic markers). If you can see the silhouette of nearby peaks, this helps rescuers triangulate.

## Build a shelter

If you do not expect rescue before dark, set up shelter. Choose a **wind-blocked, slightly elevated, no falling-rock location**. Use branches, leaves, and terrain to build a wind wall. Lay leaves and bark on the ground to insulate from cold ground (ground conducts heat far faster than air does).

## Fire serves double duty

A fire provides **warmth** and acts as a **rescue signal**. During the day, use wet leaves or pine needles to produce **thick white smoke** (high moisture, dense smoke). At night, keep visible **flames**. Three fires in a triangle or in a line is the international distress signal. Gather sufficient fuel before nightfall so it does not burn out at 3 AM.

## Whistle beats shouting

Shouting wears out vocal cords within 30 minutes and carries only a short distance. A **whistle can be blown hundreds of times and pierces forest**. The international distress signal is **3 blasts per minute** (~5 sec apart), pause 1 minute, repeat. Any response of 3 blasts in a group means rescuers are approaching.

## Phone usage strategy

Mobile phones often have no signal in mountains. **Close all apps, turn off Bluetooth, enable airplane mode** to save battery. Every 1-2 hours, climb to a higher open spot and turn the phone back on to attempt a transmission. **SMS uses less power and succeeds with weaker signal than voice** (SMS retransmits automatically). When signal is available, dial 911 / 112 with GPS coordinates (iOS / Android show coordinates even without network).

## Distress signal priority

Priority order: **mobile call 911 > satellite SOS (iPhone 14+ Emergency SOS) > large signal fire > 3-whistle blasts > mirror flash (to helicopters)**. Never give up active signaling — rescue teams investigate any signal aggressively.
